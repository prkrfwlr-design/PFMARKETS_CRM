How I think about your economics

Window cleaning is lower ticket, but a booked route is worth far more than a single job. The marketing has to build for both.

Frequency beats ticket size

A $150 monthly storefront account is worth more over a year than most one-off jobs twice its price. I aim campaigns at customers who'll say yes to a standing schedule, not just a one-time clean.

Two different buyers, one route

Commercial storefronts want reliability and a set day of the month. Residential customers want a clean window before company comes over. Same crew, different message for each.

Reviews and reminders do the heavy lifting

Because the ticket's lower, ad spend has to work harder to pay off. Email reminders and review requests keep past customers rebooking without another paid click.

Quick questions

How do you market a recurring route differently than a one-off job?

The offer itself changes. Instead of "book a window cleaning," the ad sells "book a standing quarterly clean." Once someone's on the route, email keeps them there without needing another ad click.

Should I go after commercial or residential first?

Depends on your crew size and route density, but I usually recommend anchoring on a few commercial storefront accounts first. They're steady, they refer other businesses on the same street, and they fill weekday gaps.

I already do pressure washing. Is window cleaning worth adding?

Almost always, yes. It's a natural add-on for existing customers, and the acquisition cost drops when you're upselling someone who already trusts you instead of chasing a brand new lead.
